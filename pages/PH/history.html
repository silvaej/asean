History of the Philippines

The Early Philippines

The Philippines were a Spanish province for over 300 years and were named after King Philip II of Spain (1556-1598). Now, the Philippines is a 7,000-island archipelago. However, it is believed that at the beginning of the third century, the inhabitants of Luzon Island is in contract and trading with East Asian seafarers and merchants, especially the Chinese. The Japanese also constructed a trading post in Aparri, Northern Luzon, in the 1400s.

Filipino trading with Chinese

The galleon trade between the Philippines and Mexico

Hunter-gatherers were the early inhabitants of the Philippines. People did, however, learn to cultivate between 3,000 and 2,000 BC. Rice was grown, and domesticated animals were kept. Filipinos began trading with China in the 10th century AD, and by the 12th century AD, Arab traders had arrived in the Philippines, bringing Islam with them.

Then in 1521, Ferdinand Magellan traveled across the Pacific. When he arrived in the Philippines, he claimed the islands for Spain. Magellan baptized a leader named Humabon intending to use him as a puppet ruler for the Spanish crown. Other chiefs were ordered to submit to Humabon by Magellan, but one chief, Lapu Lapu, refused. Magellan commanded a troop to annihilate him. The Spanish soldiers, on the other hand, were dispersed, and Magellan was murdered.

The Magellan and Lapu-Lapu Encounter

The Spaniards did not establish a foothold in the Philippines until 1565 when Miguel Lopez de Legazpi led an expedition to Cebu and established a fort. The Spaniards arrived in Luzon in 1571. They established the city of Intramuros (later known as Manila), which became the Philippines' capital. Luzon was captured by Spanish conquistadors who marched inland. They established a feudal order. Spaniards owned vast estates worked by Filipinos.

Manila Intramuros during Spanish Era

Friars joined the conquistadors who then converted the Filipinos to Catholicism. Schools and universities were also created by the friars. The years in the Philippines proceeded uneventfully until the British seized Manila in 1762. They kept it for two years, but under the conditions of the Treaty of Paris, signed in 1763, they hand it back in 1764.

The Philippines in the 19th Century

A rebellion sprang out in Cavite in 1872, but it was promptly put down. However, with the help of a writer named Jose Rizal (1861-1896), nationalist sentiments continued to rise. He fueled the embers of nationalism with his writings Noli Me Tangere (Touch Me Not) and El Filibusterismo (The Filibusterer). Jose Rizal founded the La Liga Filipina movement in 1892, which advocated for reform rather than revolution. Rizal was imprisoned and deported to Dapitan, Mindanao, as a result.

Members of La Liga Filipina

Meanwhile, Andres Bonifacio founded the Katipunan, a more extreme organization. They started a revolt in August 1896. Jose Rizal, who did not support the revolution but was accused of doing so, was executed on December 30, 1896. Yet his execution merely enraged Filipinos and fueled the revolution.

Execution of Rizal at Bagumbayan

Later on, Emilio Aguinaldo, head of the Katipuneros in Cavite Province drafted a constitution and formed the Republic of Biak-na-Bato in Bulacan province. In 1897, an impasse between the Spanish government and Aguinaldo arose. After discussions between the two parties, Aguinaldo accepted an amnesty from the Spaniards and US$800,000 in exchange for his exile to Hong Kong with his government.

Then in 1898 came the war between the USA and Spain. On May 1, 1898, the Americans under the command of U.S. Admiral George Dewey of the United States Navy fought the Spanish Navy in Manila Bay with the help of Emilio Aguinaldo. Faced with defeat, Spain gave the Philippines to the United States in 1898 in exchange for a payment of US$ 20 million as part of the "Treaty of Paris" that ended the Spanish-American War. Filipinos led by Emilio Aguinaldo declared independence on June 12, 1898.

The United States, which had planned to take over the colony, as opposed to this announcement. On February 4, 1899, a war broke out between American forces in Manila and the Filipinos. The Philippine-American War lasted until Aguinaldo was captured in 1902.

The Philippines in the 20th Century

American rule was paternalistic. “Benevolent Assimilation” was the name of their policy. They attempted to “Americanize” the Filipinos, but they never quite succeeded. They did, however, do some good. Many American teachers were dispatched to the Philippines on the ship Thomas, and they were successful in increasing literacy. The Philippines became a commonwealth and became semi-independent in 1935. Manuel Quezon was elected President of the Philippines. In 1945, the United States guaranteed that the Philippines would be entirely independent.

USAT Thomas Ship sent American teachers to the Philippines

Japan, on the other hand, attacked the US Navy at Pearl Harbor in December 1941. The Philippines were invaded by Japanese soldiers on December 10, 1941. On January 2, 1941, they took Manila. All of the Philippines had been under Japanese control by May 6, 1942. In October 1944, however, American troops returned to the Philippines. In February 1945, they retook Manila.

Japanese Occupation of the Philippines

The Philippines became independent on 4 July 1946. Manuel Roxas was sworn in as the first President of the Republic of the Philippines. Roxas passed away in April of 1948. Elpidio Quirino took over as his successor. Both Roxas and Quirino had to deal with the Hukbalahap, a powerful anti-Japanese guerrilla group that turned militant and tarnished the ruling class. Ramon Magsaysay finally put an end to the organization, which is Pres. Quirino's Secretary of Defense. In the 1953 elections, Magsaysay defeated Quirino. He was a popular president who was well-liked by the general public. Carlos Garcia succeeded Magsaysay after he died in an airplane disaster on March 17, 1957.

In 1965, Ferdinand E. Marcos became the first president of the Philippines to be re-elected for a second term. He launched a massive public works program and remained popular throughout his first term. Due to alleged dishonesty in the 1969 campaign, the loss in economic growth, government corruption, and decreasing peace and order, his popularity began to dwindle after his re-election. In 1972, near the end of his second and final term in government, he declared martial law. Senator Benigno "Ninoy" Aquino and Senator Jose Diokno, both staunch opponents, were among the first to be imprisoned. With the help of the military, Marcos maintained an iron grip on the country during the Martial Law years. The legislature was abolished, and opposition leaders were imprisoned.
Declaration of Martial Law in Malacañang Palace

Marcos called an election in February 1986. The opposition united behind Cory Aquino, Benigno's widow. Marcos was declared the winner (a clear case of electoral fraud). Ordinary people came to the streets to demonstrate their support for Cory Aquino, who also declared victory. Marcos' supporters deserted him, and he surrendered to the inevitable and went into exile. Corazon Aquino's life did not proceed as planned as she managed to stave off seven coup attempts. Furthermore, many Filipinos believed that they should leave the American bases in the Philippines (Subic Bay Naval Base and Clark Air Base).

EDSA Revolution

In 1992 Fidel Ramos became president. He enhanced the Philippines' infrastructure, notably the electrical supply. After the industry was privatized, the economy began to grow at a faster rate. The Philippine economy, on the other hand, had a crisis around the end of the 1990s. Meanwhile, Joseph Estrada was elected president in 1998. Estrada was impeached in November 2000 after being accused of corruption. Estrada was found not guilty. Despite this, protests against him took place, and the military withdrew its backing. Estrada was forced to resign and was replaced by Vice President Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo. In 2004, she was re-elected.

The Philippines in the 21st Century

[12] The Philippines is still poor today, but things are changing. The Philippine economy has risen at a rate of roughly 6% per year since 2010. There are reasons to be positive about the future now. In the meantime, the Philippines launched its first satellite in 2016. It was known as Diwata-1. The Philippines had a population of 106 million people in 2020.
